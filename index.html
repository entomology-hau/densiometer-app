Field protocol

Use a vertically upward image from the same camera and lens setting each time. For densiometer-style sampling, use the widest available lens or a fisheye adapter, keep exposure/focus fixed where possible, avoid direct sun glare, and keep the phone level. Take four readings around the reference point or tree and average them.

What this app calculates

The primary estimate is a 96-dot densiometer-equivalent canopy closure: dots classified as sky openings are treated as non-overstory, and dots classified as canopy are treated as occupied by overstory. A second estimate uses all sampled pixels inside the selected circular field of view.

Method notes

A standard spherical densiometer uses 24 squares subdivided into 96 imaginary dots. In this app the fixed central overlay preserves that 96-dot sampling frame while the photo can be moved and zoomed underneath it. In dense canopies, sky-opening dots are counted and converted to non-overstory area; the complement is overstory density. This app applies the same 96-dot logic to a phone image, then adds a denser pixel-area estimate for comparison.

The output is comparable within a study only when acquisition is standardised. Use the negative-image and sensitivity controls consistently within a dataset, because they change the pixel-classification decision boundary. A normal phone lens samples a narrower and differently projected field of view than a spherical mirror, so treat the result as a mobile-image analogue unless it has been calibrated against local densiometer readings or a hemispherical-photography workflow.

All image analysis runs locally in the browser. Once cached, the app works without a network connection.
